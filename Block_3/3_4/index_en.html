Better than DVD players

Your vision has the same concept as a DVD player, only more efficient. Their eyes stay on the tip of the sticks and move independently of each other. Each eye has "trinocular vision" - it can measure depth and distance by on its own, focusing on objects with three separate regions. They can see a special type of spiral light called circular polarized light that no other animal can see. And they have a structure similar to technology found in CD and DVD players, but much more effective.

                    Most people have three types of light-detecting cells, or photoreceptors, in their retinas. These are sensitive to red, green and blue light, respectively. Birds, reptiles and many fish have a fourth photoreceptor that detects ultraviolet light. Four it is enough. Mathematical models tell us that you only need four receivers, maybe five, to effectively encode the colors within that range.

                    The mantis shrimp has twelve different photoreceptors. Eight of them cover the parts of the spectrum that we can see, while four cover the ultraviolet region. That it seems a ridiculous excess. If four or five receptors are all that an animal need, "why the hell do stomatoopods need 12 channels?" says Justin Marshall, who led the new study.

                    Mantis shrimp are aggressive relatives of crabs and lobsters and attack other animals, crippling them with devastating blows. Their secret weapons are a pair of                     articulated arms folded under the head, which they can unfold at speeds incredible.

                    "Lance" species have arms ending in a diabolical and barbed spike that they use to impale soft-bodied prey like fish. But the species larger 'crushers' have arms ending in heavy clubs and use them to punch with the same force as a rifle bullet.
